Pollution and Water-quality Story

Great Barrier Reef Inshore Waters

This page is organised like a long-form infographic. It begins with the inshore monitoring sites, then moves through hotspot patterns, pollutant types, time signals, ecological response and management progress. Taken together, the official records show how runoff pressure appears across locations, seasons, indicators and management outcomes in Great Barrier Reef inshore waters.

Chapter 1

Inshore Monitoring Sites

The first chapter defines the study area. These charts are about monitored inshore waters, where river discharge and coastal runoff first appear in reef-connected water.

Inshore monitoring sites show suspended-solids hotspots clustering around selected river mouths.

Each point on the map represents one AIMS inshore water-quality monitoring site. Colour shows the percentile group of that site's median suspended-solids value, which helps separate the smaller number of high-value river-mouth sites from the more common low-to-moderate monitoring sites. Using site medians rather than single samples also reflects the more typical level of each location across the monitoring window.

Higher suspended-solids values are concentrated around selected river mouths and coastal outlets, including Hell Hole Creek, Groper Creek, Kennedy River mouth and Hull River mouth. That is consistent with how land-derived sediment enters the inshore Reef: runoff tends to arrive first through river mouths and outlet zones, so those sites are more likely to record persistently high turbidity and suspended-solids pressure.

Great Barrier Reef pollution is not a uniform film spread evenly along the coast. It first becomes visible in the inshore receiving zones connected to catchments, especially where muddy runoff is repeatedly delivered to the sea.

Because this dataset only covers inshore monitoring sites, the map compares spatial differences between monitored inshore locations rather than differences between inshore waters and offshore coral reefs. It presents relative hotspot intensity within the official inshore monitoring network, and it sets up the next point: hotspot patterns change when the indicator changes.

Chapter 2

Pollutant Types

The Reef Plan does not treat runoff as one undivided issue. It tracks nitrogen, sediment, particulate nutrients and pesticide risk separately because each creates a different management problem.

Hotspots change by indicator

Inshore water quality cannot be summarised by a single “most polluted region.” Dissolved inorganic nitrogen, suspended solids and chlorophyll a reflect different kinds of water-quality pressure, so the same region changes rank depending on which indicator is being viewed.

The reason is that the three indicators describe different processes. DIN is closer to nutrient enrichment, suspended solids to sediment and turbidity, and chlorophyll a to biological response. When the metric changes, the hotspot story changes too.

Because the three indicators use different units and value ranges, bar lengths should only be compared within the same panel, not directly across indicators. On that basis, Mackay Whitsunday stands out for suspended solids and chlorophyll a, while Fitzroy and Burdekin rank higher for dissolved inorganic nitrogen.

Great Barrier Reef inshore pollution is not a single pressure with a single hotspot. Different pollutant pathways stand out in different regions, so public discussion of the problem works better when those differences stay visible.

Reef Plan Pollutant Grades

This heatmap places the management framework directly on the page. It shows which pollutant groups are being tracked and which regions still receive weaker grades for each type of pressure.

Read it as a chapter about management categories rather than a search for one worst place. The official grades make clear that different regions are being held back by different pollutant pressures: nitrogen remains weak in several regions, sediment is especially weak in parts of the central and southern catchments, and pesticide risk is generally closer to the target except in Mackay Whitsunday.

Those differences reflect the fact that pollutants do not come from identical sources or follow identical pathways. Nutrients, sediment, particulate nutrients and pesticides are shaped by different land uses, erosion processes and management histories, so it is unsurprising that their regional grades diverge.

Reef pollution is not only about whether condition is weak or strong overall; it is also about which pollutant pathway is still holding a region back, and why that pathway remains difficult to manage.

Chapter 3

Time Signal

The next chapter asks when pressure becomes clearer. Monthly monitoring and annual context both suggest that the inshore signal strengthens alongside freshwater input, rainfall and river discharge.

Monthly DIN and Salinity

These two variables are shown together because they move in opposite directions. The chart uses monthly medians from the 2024-2025 inshore sample window, so the goal is not to describe one site or one flood event, but to show what the typical monthly signal looks like within the extracted dataset.

The pattern is fairly clear. DIN rises from about 0.35 mg/L in January to about 0.42 in February and around 0.535 in March, while salinity drops over the same period from about 34.4 PSU to around 32.2 and then 31.7. By October, DIN falls to roughly 0.13 mg/L while salinity returns to about 35.65 PSU.

This kind of pattern is consistent with seasonal freshwater input. During wetter months, more land-derived freshwater enters the inshore strip, which lowers salinity and can carry nutrient-rich runoff into coastal waters at the same time.

Pollution pressure is not static through the year. The inshore Reef is more exposed at some times than others, especially when runoff pathways are active. The chart is still only a sample-window summary rather than a full climatology, but it gives the story a clear seasonal rhythm.

Rainfall, Discharge and 2022 Condition

This chart provides the broader yearly setting. It places annual rainfall, river discharge per area and the official 2022 inshore condition grades in one frame, so the six reporting regions can be compared against a shared hydro-climatic background.

The contrasts are important. Wet Tropics combines the highest rainfall and highest discharge density yet remains moderate; Mackay Whitsunday also sits in a high-rainfall, high-discharge setting but is graded poor; Fitzroy and Burnett Mary are also poor despite lower discharge density. Rainfall clearly matters, but it does not act as a simple one-variable explanation for condition grades.

The reason is that rainfall and discharge shape exposure, but they are not the only influences on regional condition. Historical pollutant loads, catchment size, management history and ecological vulnerability also affect how weak or resilient a region appears in the official result.

Great Barrier Reef inshore pollution is not only a marine story; it is also a catchment and runoff story. The chart helps explain why the official 2022 decline was uneven across regions rather than geographically uniform.

Chapter 4

Ecological Response

Water-quality indicators matter because they change the inshore environment. This chapter links turbidity, nutrients and biological response back to visible ecological outcomes.

Suspended Solids and Secchi Depth

Secchi depth is a practical measure of how far someone can see into the water. This means the scatter plot does more than compare two variables mathematically: it translates suspended-solids pressure into a more intuitive environmental effect, namely reduced water clarity.

The cloud of points shows that higher suspended-solids values are usually associated with lower Secchi depth. At very low suspended-solids levels, sites still span a wider range of visibility outcomes. But once suspended solids move into higher ranges, the points compress toward lower clarity, including observations near zero metres.

The pattern appears because suspended particles block and scatter light. Murkier water reduces visibility, but it also reduces how much light reaches benthic habitats and shallow ecological communities.

Suspended-solids hotspots are not only numbers on a map; they can also correspond to darker, more light-limited inshore environments. The scatter is not a one-factor causal model, but the overall direction is clear.

DIN and Chlorophyll a

Chlorophyll a is commonly used as an indicator of phytoplankton biomass. This shows the biological side of the runoff story: DIN is a nutrient-input indicator, while chlorophyll a is closer to a biological response.

The scatter is not a tight straight line, but the overall direction still matters. Most samples cluster in the lower-DIN, lower-chlorophyll space. As DIN moves upward, the range of chlorophyll outcomes widens and the highest chlorophyll values appear in the higher-DIN part of the plot.

This pattern is consistent with a simple ecological explanation. When more dissolved inorganic nitrogen is available, phytoplankton have better access to a key nutrient, so higher biological activity becomes more likely when light and residence time are also suitable.

Runoff pressure does not only alter chemistry; it can also shift the biological activity of inshore waters. At the same time, light, residence time, mixing and other nutrients still matter, so the relationship is best understood as a tendency rather than a fixed rule.

Inshore Hard Coral Cover

The coral record extends the story into longer-term ecological response. It does not claim that runoff alone explains every change, but it shifts the story from short-term water-quality variation to long-run ecological trajectories between 2005 and 2022.

The regional trajectories are notably uneven. Wet Tropics rises from about 30.9 in 2005 to about 39.3 in 2022, while Burdekin increases from roughly 17.9 to 29.0. Fitzroy starts high, falls sharply by 2015, and then partially recovers by 2022. Mackay Whitsunday shows the clearest lingering decline, remaining far below its mid-2010s level by the end of the series.

The reason such patterns matter is that coral cover responds to multiple pressures at once: storms, heat stress, recruitment, macroalgae competition and water-quality conditions all shape recovery. Runoff does not explain everything, but cleaner or dirtier inshore environments can influence how well reefs recover after disturbance.

This moves the story beyond pollution readings and into long-term ecological consequence. The inshore water-quality story matters because it helps shape the conditions under which reefs recover, stall or decline. The figure still needs cautious interpretation, but it shows clearly that recovery is uneven across regions.

Chapter 5

Management Progress

The final chapter returns to policy. Official targets show that some pollutants are much closer to completion than others, while the latest condition result remains only moderate or below across all inshore regions.

Target Completion Rates

These bars convert the Reef Plan results into one comparable scale: the share of each target already completed. That conversion matters because it separates an absolute reduction value from the share of the target already achieved.

On that standardised scale, pesticides sit closest to completion at about 96%, particulate phosphorus reaches about 93%, particulate nitrogen about 77%, sediment about 64%, and dissolved inorganic nitrogen only about 47%.

The slower progress on DIN and sediment is consistent with the nature of those pressures. They are often linked to broad land use, diffuse runoff and erosion processes across large catchments, which are generally harder to reduce quickly than more targeted management issues.

This is what makes the management gap legible. The page is not only asking where pollution appears; it is also asking whether management is catching up. These are Reef-wide cumulative progress values rather than single-year concentration readings, so they show the policy position reached by June 2022 before the next chart returns to the latest official inshore condition outcome.

Latest official result

Inshore Water-quality Condition

This final card brings the story back to the present. Even where progress exists, the 2022 official inshore result still shows no region above moderate. That makes it the simplest summary of the current condition story.

The regional split is straightforward: Cape York, Wet Tropics and Burdekin are graded moderate, while Mackay Whitsunday, Fitzroy and Burnett Mary are graded poor. No region reaches good or very good.

This remains the case because regional condition is shaped by accumulated pressure, uneven ecological recovery and the fact that several pollutant categories still lag behind target. Policy progress does not immediately translate into a fully healthy inshore condition picture.

The hotspot maps, seasonal patterns, ecological relationships and target gaps all lead back here: Great Barrier Reef inshore pollution is still a live regional issue rather than a resolved one. The chart is highly summarised, but it serves as the official closing frame for the whole story.
