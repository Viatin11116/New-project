LAND RUNOFF

AND REEF WATER QUALITY

Why this matters

The Reef's inshore water is shaped by what happens upstream: runoff, discharge, nutrients, sediment and pesticide risk.

Audience

A general Australian audience that needs a readable, one-page explanation rather than an expert dashboard.

Story focus

Runoff still leaves a visible footprint on Reef water.

This page combines the latest public Reef Report Card with recent AIMS monitoring. The story is more focused than a dashboard: targets have improved in places, but inshore water still carries the signatures of runoff through sediment, salinity, clarity and chlorophyll.

Target progress at Reef-wide scale

Policy progress exists, but nitrogen and sediment are still well short of the 2025 goalposts.

Regional pressure map

Weak grades cluster by region and pollutant type.

The heatmap gives the fastest overview of where runoff pressure remains most persistent across the catchments.

Main map

Different catchments feed different parts of the Reef coast.

The map keeps the real Queensland coastline, then links inland catchment-side regions to the coastal waters they influence. Wider bands show larger discharge, warmer colour shows dirtier wet-season water, and the right-hand summary ranks the same coastal zones by wet-season suspended solids.

Official inshore water quality stayed moderate or poor

No region in the latest official condition results reached "good" or "very good" in inshore water quality.

Big discharge does not guarantee strong nitrogen gains

Wetter, high-discharge regions are not automatically the regions closest to their dissolved inorganic nitrogen target.

Suspended solids still vary sharply from site to site

Even within the same broad region, some monitored sites remain consistently murkier than others.

Wet-season water is usually dirtier than dry-season water

The seasonal contrast is one of the clearest signs that runoff pulses are still shaping inshore water.

Annual change

Annual policy gains are real, but they are usually small.

Year-on-year reductions matter, but most regions are still advancing in increments rather than leaps.

Seasonality

Salinity drops through the wet months, consistent with freshwater runoff.

Monthly medians help show when river influence reaches the coastal water column most strongly.

More suspended solids usually means poorer clarity

Murkier water and shorter Secchi depth travel together, which is exactly what a plume-affected water column should look like.

Lower salinity often travels with higher chlorophyll

Fresher inshore water frequently coincides with more chlorophyll, linking runoff conditions with biological response.

Takeaway

The land-to-sea story is still unfinished.

The combined evidence does not describe a Reef that is simply recovered or fixed. It describes a system where some targets are improving slowly, but where inshore waters still react strongly to runoff, plume conditions and wet-season discharge.
