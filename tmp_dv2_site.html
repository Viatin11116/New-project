Australia's Gaming Nation

We tell ourselves a story about who plays video games: it's teenage boys, alone in a bedroom, lost in a fantasy. The Australian data tells a different story  one of mothers and grandparents, of indie studios in Hobart, of a half-billion-dollar export industry, and of a hobby that has quietly become a defining part of national culture.

Gender

For the first time in 20 years of research, women outnumber men among Australian gamers.

Australia Plays 2025. The 3-point shift since 2023 sits within sampling error, but the long-term direction is unmistakable.

Households

Nearly nine in ten Australian homes contain at least one gaming device. Half own three or more.

Smartphones count as gaming devices in this measure  they are the platform Australians spend the most money on.

Chapter 01 ñ The players

The average gamer is 35, working full-time, and probably your colleague.

Two decades ago the gaming stereotype was reasonably accurate: most players really were young men. That picture has gone. Today, 81% of Australian players are adults. 71% are of working age. One in ten is over 65. The age distribution of the gaming population now closely mirrors the age distribution of Australia itself.

Who plays  by age and gender

Each bar represents the share of all Australian gamers in that age band and gender. The pyramid is widest in the 2544 bracket  the parents-with-kids years.

Devices

Consoles still rule. But almost everyone is also playing on a phone.

The XboxPlayStationNintendo console remains the single most-used gaming device in Australia, present in 87% of player households. But mobile gaming has overtaken the PC: more Australians game on a smartphone than at a desk, and mobile is the platform on which Australians spend the most money each year.

Players move between devices fluidly. Most use at least three, which is why the percentages here do not sum to 100.

Why they play

For most Australians, gaming is how they relax and how they feel they're getting good at something.

The motivation chart breaks the most enduring stereotype about gamers  that they are escapists, isolated, addicted. The two top reasons Australians give for playing are a sense of achievement and entertainment. Three in four say they play to manage stress; six in ten play to connect with friends or family. Only about half play to escape.

Seventy-seven percent of Australian gamers play with other people, online or in the same room.

Chapter 02 ñ The industry

Behind the players is a half-billion-dollar industry that has tripled in a decade.

The Australian Game Development Survey (AGDS) is the most rigorous measurement of Australia's local games industry. Across ten years of surveys, it has documented an extraordinary climb: from 63 studios in FY16 to 141 today, and from $115 million in revenue to $608 million.

Industry revenue, FY2016FY2025

The cyan line tracks AGDS-reported industry revenue. FY2025 is asterisked because IGEA combined survey responses with supplementary Sensor Tower data for the first time, making the headline figure not strictly comparable to prior years.

Jobs

Full-time employment has nearly tripled  and women have grown from 19% of staff to 22%.

In FY2016 there were 842 full-time game-development jobs in Australia. By FY2025 there are 2,443  and over half of surveyed studios say they plan to hire again in 2026. The workforce is also slowly diversifying: women and gender-diverse employees together accounted for under 19% of staff in FY16 and now exceed 26%.

Industry-wide, FY24 saw a small contraction during a global games downturn. FY25 shows employment holding steady while revenue surges.

The export story

Ninety-five cents in every dollar earned by an Australian games studio comes from a player overseas.

The cyan stream is revenue leaving Australia  sold to international platforms, publishers, and players. The purple stream is the comparatively tiny share sold domestically. Together they form one of the most export-oriented creative industries in the country.

Chapter 03 ñ The geography

Melbourne is the capital. But Tasmania and Canberra make the most of their size.

A simple count of studios puts Victoria first by a wide margin  50 of Australia's 141 studios are headquartered there, and more than a third of all industry jobs are in Melbourne alone. But when you adjust for population, a different geography emerges: small jurisdictions like Tasmania and the ACT have the highest density of game studios per capita in the country.

Chapter 04 ñ Inside the studios

Most Australian studios are smaller than five people. A handful do most of the revenue.

Inside the AGDS dataset, two truths sit side-by-side. Almost half of all Australian studios have five or fewer staff  the indie, garage-band end of the industry is its largest demographic. And yet a small cohort of mature studios, mostly in Melbourne and Sydney, account for the lion's share of revenue and the largest teams. The shape of the industry is a long tail.

What everyone in the industry actually does for a job

Programmers and engineers are still the largest single role, but artists are almost as numerous. Together, the two creative-technical roles account for more than half of the entire workforce.

Chapter 05 ñ The horizon

If the past decade rhymes, Australia will be a billion-dollar games exporter before the end of the decade.

The chart below projects industry revenue forward to FY2028 using the historical 10-year compound growth rate. The cone shows the range of plausible outcomes. None of this is guaranteed  projections never are  but the direction of travel is consistent: Australia is building a real, durable, globally competitive games industry, and it is doing so at the same time as a generation of its citizens has decided that playing video games is what they do for fun.

In summary

Australia is not just a country that plays games. It is becoming a serious gaming nation  culturally, in the 19 million people who pick up a controller every week; socially, in the family living rooms where parents and children play together; and economically, in the studios from Hobart to Brisbane that ship Australian-made games to a global audience.
